THE SIX

The open studio is a sort of purgatory. A space for both making, concentration, as well as stupid shit. Acting as a bridge; an intermediary link between my home in Toronto and the Studio itself.

I often find myself spending the most time on the 6th floor. It is certainly a curious thing to think about as I type this reflection upon a random lecture hall in a random building within the UofT campus during reading week. Why don’t I seek more unique spaces for work? Why don’t I work at a coffee shop? Why don’t I work at a library? Why don’t I literally seek anywhere else for work or study? Is it due to the collective and community-like nature of common designers alike? I don’t know, but I know that this place will become pivotal to the way I evolve as both a person and a designer.

AN EDUCATION

I went to school quite a bits away from my house for a majority of my life. Starting from Grade 4 a 7:30 AM commute to school that started at 8:10AM everyday quickly became a common occurrence. I still remember the first time I went on this commute and holy shit it felt twice as long then it does now. I wonder if you really psychologically become used to a commute and time passes quicker the more you take said commute. Anyhow, this was a pretty random core memory of mine.

this photo is sourced from my friend's phone. Taken at some random park.

Delta was where my highschool was located, and unlike the glorious king city that I actually resided in, full offensive, I mean it, this city was dead as hell. Delta was literally filled with 80% just 60+ retirees. The most exciting activity was either visiting an exceeding average mall or going to play golf. Neither of which, I’m afraid, I was rather fond of at the time. It didn’t even have a T&T and my friend’s parents would commonly commute to another city just to get goods. No costco either, I’m telling you I would not like to live here. A common running joke was that one of god’s greatest punishments would be to confine someone for life to this city.

Besides the city being shit, my high school, and middle school, and half of my elementary for that matter ( yes it was a co-ed K-12 private school ) was rather an enjoyable experience. Much like many other youths I would presume, the Kindergarten to Grade 6 experience was mostly smooth sailing until they start hitting you with the FSA (?) at Grade 7 then the shit picks up. Pretty much the same story here.

I LIVED IN A ROOM

Now I know that I have been glazing and hyping up this city for a while now and that is for good reason. This island city that may or may not sink due to global warming in some decades or having the “big one” hit the west coast, this island city sitting below sea level providing a dyke for a pretty scene and VERY vibey bike rides during the summer is where I grew up.

Starting off my Canadian Career at an elementary school in Richmond and in a Town House will always be a core memory for me. This was basically my intro to Canada, mixed in with the culture shock from moving from China. It certainly populates several synapses in my mind and will continue to do so.

this photo is sourced from my phone. Taken in my office at home.

THE FOOD IS BUSSIN I DARESAY

I really like the street food they have here and I think this was one of the first places that I visited outside of my city since I was born.

I have family relatives here, and I remember vividly chilling at my cousin’s place and him introducing me to some “three kingdom” type fighting game and 2K. I was baffled bro, totally engrossed in that shit. I blame him for any crippling video addiction, also I blame my dad cause he introduced me to Plants vs Zombies that was also a core memory.

I remember in the summer you can just go onto the street and pull up to one of the many stalls outside and ask for something I believe was like lamb noodle soup? Not sure as to the specifics but with 5 RMB you could get a pretty nice and rather delicious bowl. I remember the plastic stools, and I remember how they would staple some sort of wrapping to their plastic tables too. Because chili oil would get everywhere and I remember the shopkeepers coming around and wiping down the tables and whatnot.

43 C

It is hot as hell here in the summer since it's located in a valley, and all of the heat gets trapped in it. 40+ degree summers were literally the norm, if you weren’t in an A/C’ed room at least every 30 minutes you might be going to the hospital with a heat stroke, and it was so hot you can literally SEE the lines. You know how in movies in the desert or hot as hell scenes they have that cliche wavy effect? Imagine that but in real life, with stagnant winds, it feels suffocating bro.
